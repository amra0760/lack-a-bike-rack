Going somewhere but not finding anywhere to securely park your bike?


Find Out WHY!

It's not your fault!

Bicycle parking still remains an issue despite the fact that bicycles have been around for centuries. However, civilians and cyclists are not the ones to blame, lack of secure on-street bicycle parking is though.


This issue is more important than it seems since when consumers can not find somewhere safe to place their bikes, they tend to rely on other methods to leave their bikes unsafely attached to any type of standing surface. This will most likely be a tree, a parking post, mailbox or some other type of lighting post or even at times a fire hydrant. This not only is a nuisance to other residents and commuters, but also hazardous, and for some objects like a tree is illegal, as there is a $1,000 dollar fine for parking a bike on a tree.


This website serves to inform and raise awareness about this issue, as well as preview locations of bike racks nearby the user’s destination. This hopefully will change the minds of commuters to possibly lead them to advocate for this issue as well possibly including cycling in their daily methods of commuting.

LEGAL Facts!

Section 2-10 Part D of the High Rules from the NYCDOT states the following: “Notwithstanding any inconsistent provision of these or any other rules, the Commissioner may issue a permit for a period of one year for temporary structures placed on sidewalks for security purposes. Such structures shall include, but not be limited to, concrete barricades, large planters and fencing”(Highway Rules, 2017)

Translation:

A bike rack (a structure built for security purposes) can not be randomly placed by a consumer on a sidewalk since the sidewalk is considered city property. They must first apply for a permit at the DOT Office of Permit Management and follow the Zoning Resolution (Bike Parking, n.d.). Simply put: as helpful as it might seem, a consumer can not simply place a bike rack on the street.


Why Cycling/bike rack?

Benefits


1

At its most basic level, bike parking encourages people to cycle. If a person chooses to ride a bike and at their destination they find the bike parking is unavailable or inconvenient, they may choose another option, like driving alone.


2

In order for cycling to compete with other modes of transportation, it must be convenient and efficient to ride and to park your bike.


3

Bike racks provide additional parking capacity for customers to visit local businesses. Bicycle parking not only invites cyclists in, but shows the business values sustainability, which is an increasingly important factor in the decisions of consumers.


4

Good bike parking benefits the disabled. By providing adequate, well-planned bike parking, business owners or property managers can ensure that handrails and ramps intended for accessibility purposes are not clogged with bicycles looking for a bike parking spot.


5

Pedestrians also benefit when orderly and aesthetic bike parking is provided. Not only does it improve the appearance of the area, it ensures that sidewalks and benches intended for pedestrians are not cluttered by bikes that do not have a designated parking space. In this way, bike parking can also prevent damage to other street furniture like garbage cans, posts, benches and trees.



Bicycle racks are only as useful, however, as they are available. Where they are not available, a suitable substitute, and a favorite among New Yorkers, is the ubiquitous green drive rail (the steel channel shaped pole) to which curbside parking regulation signs are attached.
It is not uncommon to see bikes locked to newspaper vending boxes, trash receptacles and sometimes even the traffic signal control boxes that are typically clustered together at many New York City street corners.

Casey Neistat

A long time advocate and unfortunately a very experienced journalist of the results of this issue is New York-based filmmaker: Casey Neistat. Throughout his past Mr. Neistat has had several of his bikes stolen due to lack of secure bike racks and so he resulted in advocating for this issue by posting several videos which have all went viral and made a statement. His first video was posted in 2005 after years of having countless bikes and bike parts stolen. Seeing that this was a chronic issue, he decided to test the system just to see how easy it was to steal a bike. Watch this video at 'Bike Thief High Resolution'

In 2012, Neistat decided to conduct another self-experiment again to see how easy it would be to steal a bike, since he still had numerous bikes being stolen from him. In this test he was purposely looking extra suspicious and trying to get attention so someone, especially a cop, would stop him. Having had three bikes successfully ‘stolen’ and more than 50 witnesses walking past him, (having even stole one in front of a precinct!) a group of policemen finally came to stop him. When he questioned them to see how many bike thieves have they have caught in their careers, they all responded with zero (Bike Theft, 2012). Watch this video at 'Bike Thief 2012'. Feel free to also read about this in his NY Times article at this following link

The following year, in 2013, Casey even went on to install a bike rack of his own! 'Stolen Bike in NYC'
